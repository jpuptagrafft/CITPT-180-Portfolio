Creating & Modifying a Video Game Engine

One of my classes during my college career was Game Engine Design, where we were instructed in the ways of creating a Game Engine. During which, within the class time, we created a very basic engine in C and C++, which allowed for the User to jump infinitely, shoot balls indefinitely, have basic collision, and basic, mainly stationary AI. For the final Project of the class, I was instructed to modify the existing game engine.
I modified the engine by restricting the movement of the player (Only allowing for the player to jump once), Created new ways to shoot in the engine, Created a Weapons and Ammo System, locking away the new shooting options until the weapon was found in the world, locking it away again if the weapon ran out of ammo, and allowing the switching to said shooting types, as well as new basic AI for movement, and “Fake” walls.

Can you see what Eye see? Tracing Axons through the layers of a Mouse’s Retina

During our Image Processing Class, we were instructed to assist in the research of one of our teachers, who was at the time studying different parts of the eyes of mice. With this in mind, we were given multiple different pictures in sequence, each representing a different slice of the eye, and each picture being about 9 MB in size. Originally, the teacher had to trace each cell by hand by flipping through layers, however, for certain cells, such as the Axon, the teacher theorized that this could be automated… and that’s where I step in.
The tracing process was done with code written in Processing3, which is an IDE, and the tracing code used modifications so it could use C++-esque code, which made it an excellent base to start from. Processing3 itself was very nice, as it meant that I didn’t have to load the entire picture below the previous picture; I just needed to load certain segments, which made it rather convenient.
